Discoveries & Insights

This was my attempt at creating a graph that would visualize how many areas were classified into each grade. As you can see, this graph has the number of grades (into the tens and hundreds of thousands) that were given out by the grades received. It can be observed that the graph itself is weighted towards the right, with the majority of grades given being either C or D by a large margin. This distribution suggests a systemic bias in historical redlining practices and reflects how a majority of areas graded were stigmatized and likely negatively affected by the grading system given that we know from records that race and socioeconomic status were heavy factors in the process.

This visualization is a shaded bar graph showing the percentage of single-family housing in Massachusetts municipalities. Through it, we can see how the areas that tend to be more expensive in terms of housing (e.g. Salem) tend to also have a higher percentage of single-family housing. This, combined with the fact that we know that race and socioeconomic status are closely tied together, suggests that those areas with more single-family homes may be affected by exclusionary housing practices.
