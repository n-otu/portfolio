Critique for NY Times Covid-19 Visualization

Here, I will be critiquing the NY Times visualization depicting the number cases of the Covid-19 Omicron variant in the United States through 2020 and 2021. To start with, this unique design does a few things well; its spiral nature makes it easy to compare the relative amount of cases during the same month and time of year across both years, and it's easy to see the ebb and flow of cases rising and falling over time. The radial grid lines, shading, and labels help clearly lay out the timeline for any prospective readers.

However, there are some areas where the design falls short. For example, the spiraling layout and lack of numerical reference for magnitude (apart from the small portion on top) make it hard to clearly understand the actual numbers and range being shown. And even the small reference at the top (showing the thickness difference between 0 and 150 thousand cases) is held separate from the spiral at the top left, and doesn’t really work well visually in terms of placement and working as an actual reference for the numbers being shown.

Final Writeup

Through my visualizations, I am trying to convey a story of the ebb and flow of a pandemic across time. I don’t just want the viewer to look at 2020 and 2021 as separate years and events, but as two mirrored parts of an overarching pandemic that came and went in waves. This is why I experimented with more radial patterns and lines, as while bars and other sectional graphs can show change over time I wanted to create something that directly compared similar months and seasons across both years for readers to view simultaneously.

I experimented with representing contrasting time periods with cool and warm-toned colors, so I paired orange and blue as well as blue and green in order to visually demarcate the years to compensate for them being physically overlaying each other. I also used shades of yellow to red to evoke a color-scale seen in nature, to help make sure viewers could instinctively contrast periods of time. I also tried to address the issue of scale I critiqued in the original visualization, and provided grids and tick marks on the charts themselves so as to serve as a useful reference for magnitude. Through these decisions, I was able to effectively show how the number of confirmed cases changed throughout the course of the year when comparing 2020 and 2021, and I believe that I was able to effectively address my other main issue of a proper scale of reference for magnitude not being present as well. Although I will say that in focusing on these factors that I’ve highlighted I did lose the unique style and appeal of the NY Times design, which may lessen its impact among audiences. My radial chart, although working off of a similar principle and idea as the spiral, may be confusing for viewers unfamiliar with that type of design.
